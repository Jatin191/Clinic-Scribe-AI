SAMPLE DOCTOR-PATIENT CONSULTATION TRANSCRIPT
Duration: ~2.5 minutes
Date: April 23, 2026

[Doctor enters the room]

Doctor: Good morning! I'm Dr. Sharma. How are you doing today?

Patient: Not great, honestly. I've been feeling really unwell for the past four days.

Doctor: I'm sorry to hear that. Can you tell me what's been bothering you most?

Patient: It started with a really bad sore throat — like, painful to swallow. Then I got a fever. I've been checking my temperature at home and it's been around 38.8 to 39.2 degrees Celsius. I also have this splitting headache and I just feel exhausted all the time.

Doctor: Any cough or runny nose?

Patient: No, actually, that's what's weird. No cough at all, no runny nose. Just the throat, fever, and headache.

Doctor: Have you noticed any swelling in your neck?

Patient: Yes! Actually yes. My wife noticed my neck looked a bit puffy on both sides. And it's tender when I press it.

Doctor: Any rash anywhere on your body?

Patient: Hmm... actually, yes. Yesterday I noticed a kind of rough, sandpapery rash on my chest and stomach. It's not itchy but it's definitely there.

Doctor: Okay. Any nausea or vomiting?

Patient: A little nausea, yes. No vomiting though.

Doctor: Any sick contacts? Anyone around you been unwell?

Patient: My 8-year-old daughter had a sore throat about a week ago. She recovered quickly though.

Doctor: Alright. Any medical history I should know about? Any allergies?

Patient: I'm generally healthy. No major conditions. I'm allergic to penicillin — I get hives with it.

Doctor: Good to know, very important. Any medications currently?

Patient: Just paracetamol for the fever. Nothing else.

Doctor: Let me examine you now.

[Examination begins]

Doctor: Opening your mouth wide for me... Yes, I can see significant erythema of the posterior pharynx, and there is tonsillar enlargement bilaterally with what appears to be white exudates on both tonsils. 

Palpating your neck... yes, there is bilateral tender cervical lymphadenopathy, most prominent in the anterior chain.

Let me check your temperature... 38.9 degrees. Heart rate is 102 beats per minute, blood pressure 118 over 76, respiratory rate 18, oxygen saturation 98% on room air.

Looking at the rash on your chest... yes, this has a classic sandpaper texture, blanches on pressure, with a circumoral pallor and what looks like a Pastia's lines in the skin folds. This is consistent with scarlatiniform rash.

Doctor: Based on your symptoms and examination, I strongly suspect you have Group A Streptococcal pharyngitis — what people commonly call Strep throat — with the associated scarlet fever rash. The clinical picture is quite classic: the exudative tonsillitis, the fever, the lymphadenopathy, and especially that sandpaper rash.

Patient: Is that serious?

Doctor: It's very treatable. Since you're allergic to penicillin, I'll prescribe you Azithromycin — a Z-pack — 500 mg on day one, then 250 mg once daily for four more days. I'd also like to do a rapid strep test today to confirm, and we'll send a throat culture as well.

Patient: What about my daughter?

Doctor: Good question. Since she had a sore throat recently, it's worth having your family doctor check her too. Strep can spread within households.

Doctor: Take plenty of rest, stay well hydrated, continue paracetamol for fever and pain relief. You should start feeling significantly better within 48 hours of starting the antibiotics. I want you to come back or contact us if you develop difficulty breathing, severe neck stiffness, drooling, or if you're not improving after 48 hours on antibiotics.

Patient: Okay, thank you so much, Doctor.

Doctor: You're welcome. Take care and feel better soon!

[End of consultation]
